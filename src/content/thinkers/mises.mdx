Ludwig von Mises is the architect of twentieth-century Austrian economics. He developed the systematic treatment of the tradition, fixed its methodological foundations, and produced — in 1920 — one of the most important predictions in the history of the social sciences.

That prediction was the **economic calculation argument**. In *Die Wirtschaftsrechnung im sozialistischen Gemeinwesen* (1920), Mises showed that a socialist economy cannot compute the rational allocation of the means of production. Without markets for capital goods, there are no prices for capital goods; without prices, there is no common unit of valuation by which to compare one production plan to another; without that unit, planning is not optimisation but arbitrary preference. The twentieth century's greatest economic experiment — the socialist calculation debate, and then the collapse of the Soviet Union — confirmed Mises in detail.

Mises's second contribution is **praxeology**: the axiomatic method he elaborated in *Nationalökonomie* (1940) and, in its definitive form, *Human Action* (1949). Starting from the axiom that man acts — a proposition *a priori* true because its denial is itself an action — Mises derives the whole of catallactics. This is not mathematical economics, and it makes no claim to empirical predictive power in the sense of physics; it is, rather, a scientific treatment of the necessary logical structure of any economy whatsoever.

His third contribution is the **regression theorem** of money (*The Theory of Money and Credit*, 1912), which solves the chicken-and-egg puzzle of money's value by tracing any medium of exchange back, via regress, to an earlier period in which the commodity was valued for non-monetary reasons.

## Key works

- *The Theory of Money and Credit* (1912).
- *Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth* (1920).
- *Socialism: An Economic and Sociological Analysis* (1922; English 1936).
- *Human Action* (1949).
