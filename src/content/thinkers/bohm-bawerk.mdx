Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk built Austrian capital theory. His three-volume *Kapital und Kapitalzins* (1884–1912) — translated as *Capital and Interest* — remains the most rigorous treatment of the subject produced in the tradition. Two of its arguments matter above all.

First, interest is not exploitation; it is the price of time. Böhm-Bawerk's pure time-preference theory locates interest in the inter-temporal valuation of present versus future goods: a present good is, *ceteris paribus*, valued more highly than an otherwise identical future good. The interest rate reflects this universal fact of human action, and is logically prior to any monetary system. The Marxian account of surplus value — which requires interest to be extracted from labour — collapses once the time-preference origin is recognised.

Second, production is *roundabout*. A capital-rich economy does not produce consumer goods directly; it produces tools that produce tools that produce consumer goods. The length of this production structure is itself an economic variable — longer structures are more productive but also more fragile, and they depend on the savings required to sustain the roundabout detour. This is the mechanical foundation on which Mises and Hayek later built Austrian business-cycle theory.

## Key works

- *Capital and Interest* (3 vols., 1884–1912).
- *Karl Marx and the Close of His System* (1896) — the demolition of the Marxian value theory.
- *The Positive Theory of Capital* (1889) — the constructive companion to the critique.
