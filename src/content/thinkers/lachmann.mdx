Ludwig Lachmann was the tradition's great theorist of capital heterogeneity and subjective expectations. *Capital and Its Structure* (1956) remains the essential statement of his position: capital goods are not a homogeneous fund of "capital," measurable in some common unit and substitutable at will. They are specific, heterogeneous, and form *structures* — assemblies of complementary goods whose value depends on the plans in which they are embedded.

This insight has radical consequences. It dissolves the Cambridge capital controversies by showing that the neoclassical aggregate production function is an artefact, not a description. It grounds the Austrian theory of the business cycle in a way that Mises and Hayek had gestured at but never fully explored: the cluster of entrepreneurial errors in a boom is not mere misallocation of a fungible capital stock; it is the construction of capital *structures* that presuppose interest rates inconsistent with the economy's true time-preference, structures that *cannot* be preserved through the bust because they were never coherent.

Lachmann is also the tradition's great theorist of **subjective expectations**. Unlike rational-expectations economics, which imposes a shared probability distribution over a known state space, Lachmann takes expectations to be genuinely subjective, dispersed, and open to radical revision. The economy on this view is *kaleidic* — in perpetual reformation — and equilibrium is a special case at best.

## Key works

- *Capital and Its Structure* (1956).
- *The Legacy of Max Weber* (1971).
- *Macro-economic Thinking and the Market Economy* (1973).

## In Praxeos

Lachmann's heterogeneous-capital insight underwrites the visual grammar of `time-preference-forest` — no two trees are the same, and the collapse of an intervention-driven boom destroys specific structures rather than a homogeneous "stock." His kaleidic framing informs the dissolution-and-reformation pattern of the `teleology` homepage sketch.
