Carl Menger founded the Austrian school in 1871 with *Grundsätze der Volkswirthschaftslehre* — *Principles of Economics* — one of the three simultaneous marginalist revolutions (alongside Jevons in England and Walras in Switzerland). Menger's contribution was to locate economic value in subjective individual valuation, not in objective labour cost, land rent, or the cost of production.

From this premise everything else unfolds. Prices are not measures of intrinsic worth but artifacts of preference rankings meeting at exchange. Demand arises not from mean utility but from the utility of the *last* unit available to a given valuer — the marginal unit. This insight dissolved the diamond-water paradox that had troubled political economy since the eighteenth century and set the course of modern price theory.

Menger's second great contribution was *On the Origins of Money* (1892), which accounted for money's emergence as a spontaneous-order phenomenon. The most saleable commodity in a barter network — typically one with favourable divisibility, durability, and recognisability — is accepted first as a medium of exchange by a handful of participants; its acceptability begets further acceptability; the process converges on a universal medium without any central decree. Mises's regression theorem of 1912 built directly on this.

## Key works

- *Principles of Economics* (1871) — the founding text.
- *Investigations into the Method of the Social Sciences* (1883) — the *Methodenstreit* polemic against the German historicist school.
- *On the Origins of Money* (1892) — the evolutionary account of money.

## In Praxeos

Menger is the first thinker the project engages. His subjective-value principle is the premise of every pricing interactive; his origin-of-money theory anchors `halving-garden` and the planned `menger-origin-of-money` module.
