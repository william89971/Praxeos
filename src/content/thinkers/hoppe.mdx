Hans-Hermann Hoppe is the tradition's principal late-twentieth-century methodologist. His *Theory of Socialism and Capitalism* (1989) and the essays collected in *The Economics and Ethics of Private Property* (1993) extended Mises's praxeological method into political philosophy, most notably through the **argumentation ethics** programme: the attempt to derive a libertarian ethical framework from the performative presuppositions of argumentation itself.

Hoppe's distinctive economic contribution — the application of Böhm-Bawerkian time-preference analysis to political regimes — appears in *Democracy: The God That Failed* (2001). His thesis: different political regimes cultivate different time preferences. Owners of inheritable capital — even absolute monarchs — have an incentive to preserve the capital value of their holdings; rotating officeholders in mass-franchise democracies have an incentive to extract present value at the expense of future value. The time-preference argument against democratic majoritarianism is thus, on Hoppe's account, economic rather than merely political.

Praxeos cites Hoppe for his technical contributions — argumentation ethics and the time-preference analysis of regimes — and treats his broader political writing as downstream of those, not as editorial canon. Readers encountering Hoppe for the first time are directed to the academic essays rather than the book-length polemics.

## Key works

- *A Theory of Socialism and Capitalism* (1989).
- *The Economics and Ethics of Private Property* (1993).
- *Democracy: The God That Failed* (2001).
