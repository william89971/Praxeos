Joseph Salerno is the principal scholar of the reconstructive generation of Austrian economics — the generation that recovered Mises from the Langean distortions which had come, by the mid-twentieth century, to dominate the standard textbook account of the socialist calculation debate.

The textbook account, established by Oskar Lange and Abba Lerner in the 1930s, held that Mises's 1920 argument was a *practical* one — socialist planners would face *computational* difficulty — and that the difficulty could in principle be overcome by a simulated market with trial-and-error price adjustment. Salerno's 1990 article, *Ludwig von Mises as Social Rationalist*, demonstrates that this was a misreading: Mises's argument was that socialism lacked a *common unit of valuation*, a point logically prior to computation. No amount of simulated pricing can recover what is absent at the level of ontology.

This reconstruction matters. It rescues the 1920 argument from the claim — still widespread in mainstream economics — that the twentieth century somehow "answered" Mises. Salerno shows that mainstream economics in fact failed to understand him. *Money, Sound and Unsound* (2010) extends the reconstructive project to monetary theory, and his editorial work on *Man, Economy, and State* established the Scholar's Edition as the canonical text.

## Key works

- *Ludwig von Mises as Social Rationalist* (1990, article).
- *Money, Sound and Unsound* (2010).
- Editor, Scholar's Edition of *Man, Economy, and State* (2004).
