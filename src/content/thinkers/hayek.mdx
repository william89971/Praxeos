F. A. Hayek is the tradition's great theorist of knowledge and coordination. His career spans three major projects: the technical economics of the 1930s, the political theory of the 1940s–60s, and the systematic philosophy of *Law, Legislation and Liberty* (1973–79).

Technically, Hayek extended Mises's business-cycle argument in *Prices and Production* (1931) and *Profits, Interest, and Investment* (1939), introducing the Hayekian triangle as a visualisation of the economy's capital structure. His 1945 article in the *American Economic Review*, *The Use of Knowledge in Society*, then reframed the entire economic problem as one not of allocating *known* resources to *known* ends, but of coordinating the **dispersed, tacit, locally-owned knowledge** that no central observer can aggregate. Prices, on this account, are not measurements — they are *signals*: compressed, decentralised pieces of information that let millions of distinct minds adjust their plans without needing to share any common store of facts. The informational function of prices is what Hayek showed the socialist calculation debate had been missing.

Politically, *The Road to Serfdom* (1944) traced the unintended consequences of central economic planning; *The Constitution of Liberty* (1960) theorised the rule of law as constraint on arbitrary power; *Law, Legislation and Liberty* (1973–79) distinguished *cosmos* (grown, spontaneous orders like language, common law, markets) from *taxis* (made, designed orders like factories and bureaucracies) and argued that attempting to govern cosmos as if it were taxis produces incoherence.

Hayek received the Nobel Memorial Prize in 1974.

## Key works

- *Prices and Production* (1931).
- *The Road to Serfdom* (1944).
- *The Use of Knowledge in Society* (1945).
- *The Constitution of Liberty* (1960).
- *Law, Legislation and Liberty* (3 vols., 1973–79).
