Israel Kirzner was the last student of Mises at New York University and the principal twentieth-century theorist of entrepreneurship within the Austrian tradition. His *Competition and Entrepreneurship* (1973) reframed the entrepreneur as neither inventor nor capitalist nor manager but as a specific cognitive agent: the person *alert* to previously unnoticed opportunities for profit.

This is philosophically radical. The profit opportunity, on Kirzner's account, was already there — unexploited, available, visible in principle. The entrepreneur is the one who *sees* it. Innovation is not strictly required; discovery is. The market process, far from being a walk toward Walrasian equilibrium along some known path, is constituted by the ongoing acts of entrepreneurial alertness that continuously discover and correct prior misallocations.

The corollary is that neoclassical equilibrium models, which assume away the informational problem that entrepreneurship exists to solve, necessarily mischaracterise the market. Markets are not states; they are processes — Lachmann's phrase — and the process is irreducibly entrepreneurial.

Kirzner received the 2006 Global Award for Entrepreneurship Research.

## Key works

- *Market Theory and the Price System* (1963).
- *Competition and Entrepreneurship* (1973).
- *Discovery and the Capitalist Process* (1985).
