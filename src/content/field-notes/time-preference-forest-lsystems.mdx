I spent two days trying to make the Time Preference Forest look like
something other than a pile of computer-generated trees. The problem was
self-inflicted: I had picked L-systems for the branching rules before
picking the register.

Lindenmayer systems are beautiful in the abstract — a grammar that rewrites
symbols, each symbol a turtle instruction — and unsparing in practice.
They produce shapes that read immediately as *generated*. An 18th-century
natural-philosophy woodcut they are not.

<Marginalia label="What worked">
  Per-segment angular jitter of ±2.9°, taper to 0.7× per recursion level,
  and a strict black-on-warm-cream palette with no anti-aliased fill.
  Those three together ate most of the algorithmic tell.
</Marginalia>

What worked in the end was three small changes compounded. One, angular
jitter on every segment — a half-degree or so, enough to break the
perfectly-equal branching that marks generated work. Two, a variable
stroke weight that tapers outward along each branch. Three, a deliberately
*coarse* base palette: no gradients, no soft edges, no colour except the
single orange that appears only during intervention. The result reads as
ink on paper because it is technically stroked rather than filled.

The correction cascade was the hard bit. Eight seconds of choreographed
collapse, with orange dissipation in the first quarter, artificial-growth
blackening through the next half, and the trees falling in the last
quarter. The pull was to make the fall instant — users expect reward
quickly. I fought that instinct. The point of the cascade is the lag: that
you know the fall is coming, and you watch it commit. The animation is
lifted straight from Rothbard's ABCT timeline, where the malinvestments
wait to be liquidated until the credit stops.

The mycorrhizal network was an accident — I had wanted to link neighbouring
root tips just to show capital complementarity, and it turned out to also
quietly solve a composition problem where the forest felt sparse at low
time preference. A Lachmannian solution to a pictorial difficulty. I'll
take it.
