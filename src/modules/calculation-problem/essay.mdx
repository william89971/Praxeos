In 1920 Ludwig von Mises published a short paper in the *Archiv für
Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik* that did something rare in the history of
economics. It settled an argument, in advance of the empirical material that
would confirm the settlement, about whether a kind of economy could exist.

That paper — *Die Wirtschaftsrechnung im sozialistischen Gemeinwesen*,
translated as *Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth* — made a
single philosophical observation. A socialist economy, Mises argued, cannot
compute. It cannot compare one production plan against another. It cannot
determine which of several available techniques for producing a given good
uses scarce means most economically. Not because planners are stupid, nor
because data processing is slow, but because there is no *common unit of
valuation* with which to compare the alternatives. Without markets for
capital goods, there are no prices for capital goods. Without prices for
capital goods, a planner faced with the choice between recipe A (which needs
ten units of steel and two of oil) and recipe B (which needs three units of
steel and fifteen of oil) has no non-arbitrary way to choose. Either answer
is a guess.

The simulation above encodes this point directly. Both panels begin with the
same consumers, the same producers, the same primary resources, the same
recipes. The only difference is how goods are allocated. In the market panel,
prices emerge through local bilateral exchange: producers post asks,
consumers choose by Cobb-Douglas preference weighted against price,
producers adjust their asks upward when they sell out and downward when
inventory piles up. These are not cosmic truths; they are adaptive signals
that converge, noisily, on the relative scarcities of everything at once.

In the planned panel there are no prices. The central planner observes only
aggregate resource supply and the set of possible techniques. When a good
can be produced by more than one recipe, the planner must pick one. It does
so, in this model, by a hash of the current tick and the producer's id —
the simulation's direct encoding of Mises's 1920 insight. The choice is
**deterministic** (a planner does not pick by flipping coins in public), but
**economically meaningless** (any other recipe would have been equally
defensible in the absence of a common valuation). Input demand from the
chosen recipes collides with finite input supply; producers are rationed;
output falls below capacity; consumers are allocated what remains by fair
share. Shortages pile up. Surpluses decay.

  Where there is no market, there is no price system; and where there is no
  price system, there can be no economic calculation.
</PullQuote>

Drag the complexity slider. As the number of goods rises, the market panel
continues to coordinate — a bit slower, noisier, with more edges on the
supply graph, but still coherent. The planned panel's waste grows faster
than the growth of its coordinating apparatus. The Misesian point is exactly
here: adding a good to a market economy is a local problem. Adding a good to
a planned economy is a global one, because every arbitrary recipe choice
makes the next arbitrary recipe choice more costly.

In the decades after 1920, Oskar Lange and Abba Lerner published a sequence
of responses that argued Mises had misunderstood his own argument. A
"simulated market," they claimed, could reproduce what actual prices do.
<SmallCaps>joseph salerno</SmallCaps>'s 1990 reconstruction, *Ludwig von Mises
as Social Rationalist*, showed that the Lange–Lerner solution had smuggled
the market back in: its trial-and-error price adjustment presupposes
precisely the distributed knowledge that Mises said socialism lacked.
Lange's simulated planner was, in the relevant sense, a market. The
literature for much of the twentieth century obscured this. The collapse of
the Soviet economy in 1989–91 was what finally convinced the textbooks.

  <SmallCaps>f. a. hayek</SmallCaps>'s *The Use of Knowledge in Society*
  (1945) reframes Mises's argument in terms of distributed, tacit,
  locally-owned knowledge. Prices are the signal mechanism that lets this
  knowledge coordinate economic activity without any central observer.
</Marginalia>

The simulation's third button invites the reader to try. Enter planner
mode. You will be shown a set of allocation choices — which recipe for each
good, how much of each primary resource to direct where. Make your picks.
Watch your decision queue lengthen as goods multiply. Watch the market
panel, visible beside you, coordinate quietly while you struggle. The
failure is not a moral failing on your part. It is the exact failure Mises
described, experienced first-hand.

A last caveat. The simulation is a model, not a proof. It cannot establish
Mises's conclusion; it can only make it *visible*. The proof lives in the
reasoning: that valuation requires a common unit, that a common unit for
producer goods can arise only from markets in those goods, that a socialist
economy by definition lacks those markets, and therefore that its planner,
however brilliant, must pick among alternatives without the apparatus that
would let the picking be economic. The twentieth century supplied the
empirical confirmation. The simulation supplies something prior to
empiricism: the experience of trying, and the look of the thing.
