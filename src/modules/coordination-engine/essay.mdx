An economy looks, from the outside, like an enormous coordinated act. Tens of millions of strangers — none of whom have met, none of whom share a plan — succeed every morning in producing breakfast. Wheat that was planted in a country none of them visited becomes bread on a table belonging to none of them. The astonishing thing about this is not that it sometimes fails. It is that it ever works.

What lets it work, on the Austrian view, is *money*. Not money in the trivial sense of the medium that hands over the bread, but money in the deeper sense: the shared unit in which every participant's plan is denominated, and through which every participant's plan can be compared with every other.

## The signal that has to be shared

Hayek's 1945 paper put the point as starkly as anyone has. <Citation n={1}>Hayek, *The Use of Knowledge in Society*, AER 35(4).</Citation> The economic problem is not the question of how to allocate given resources to known ends. It is the question of how to use knowledge that no single mind possesses. Each participant knows a tiny piece — the local price of the harbour, the morning's catch, the reliability of a particular foreman. The price system is the network through which these tiny pieces become a coordinated whole.

The engine above you is what that network feels like in three dimensions. Each agent is one participant; each edge is one voluntary relationship through which prices flow. At sound money — the slider near zero — every pulse locks to a shared phase. The network breathes together. *That is the coordination*. There is nothing in any individual agent that is doing it; it lives in the coherence between them.

## What "sound money" means

The phrase "sound money" is older than Austrian economics, but the Austrians give it the most exact content. Money is sound when its purchasing power is determined by the same forces — supply, demand, the labour of producing it — that determine the purchasing power of any other commodity. <Citation n={2}>Mises, *Human Action*, ch. XVII.</Citation> When the unit of account is itself a politically administered variable, every price quoted in it inherits the politics of its administration.

The technical word for what you see decohere as the slider rises is *signal*. At low distortion, the message that travels the network — *I am willing to pay this much for that* — arrives at receivers as a meaningful comparison. At high distortion, the same nominal message means different things to different receivers, because each one is interpreting it through a different recent experience of the unit of account. The network does not break in any obvious way. It simply stops being able to carry shared meaning.

The most significant fact about this system is the economy of knowledge with which it operates, or how little the individual participants need to know in order to be able to take the right action.
</PullQuote>

## The Cantillon edge

Rothbard's *Man, Economy, and State* extends the analysis at the edges. <Citation n={3}>Rothbard, *Man, Economy, and State*, ch. 11.</Citation> New money does not enter every account simultaneously. It is created and lent at particular points — banks, governments, favoured borrowers — and from those points it propagates outward through chains of further spending. Receivers near the source experience the new purchasing power before prices have adjusted. Receivers far from the source experience higher prices before they receive any of the new money.

This is the *Cantillon effect*, named after the 18th-century banker Richard Cantillon, who first described the mechanism. In the engine above, you see it as the asymmetry of breakage at high distortion: certain edges fail before others, and the agents at the periphery of the new-money flow experience the network as broken before the agents at its centre do.

## Lachmann's reservation

Lachmann would caution against reading the engine as a model that *converges*. <Citation n={4}>Lachmann, *The Market as an Economic Process*, ch. III.</Citation> What you see at low distortion is not equilibrium; it is the running pattern of partial coordination that obtains when the signal is good. Even with perfectly sound money, every individual plan is fallible, every signal partial, every pulse incomplete. The market process is open-ended. Coordination is a continuous achievement, not a destination.

What sound money preserves is not certainty. It is *legibility*. The pulses still arrive incomplete; they just arrive meaning the same thing.

## The implication

The engine above you is meant to make one claim feelable: that coordination at the scale of an economy is not a lucky accident, and not a public good provided by anyone in particular. It is a property of a network that is allowed to use a stable common signal. Distort the signal and what looked like a coordinated whole turns out to have been the synchrony.

*Homo agit*. Man acts. He acts, when he acts, by reading a price. When the prices are honest, his actions add up. When they are not, they cancel.
