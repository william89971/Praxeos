In 1920, Ludwig von Mises published a short essay that, for a few decades, was treated as an embarrassment to the profession. *Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth* argued that a fully socialised economy — one in which the means of production are owned in common — cannot compute. Not for ideological reasons, not because socialists were lazy or corrupt, but because the medium in which economic computation happens, the medium of *prices*, is unavailable to it.

The argument is unusual because it operates at a higher altitude than most political economy. It is not a complaint about incentives. It is not a worry about freedom. It is an argument about the *mathematical possibility* of a certain kind of decision.

## What is to be calculated

A modern economy is not one decision. It is millions of decisions, all of them entangled. Should this iron go to the railway or the factory? Should that labour-hour repair the harbour or build the school? Should the timber be felled now or left to grow? Each of these choices interacts with every other through chains of substitution and complementarity that no single mind can hold.

The market handles this through prices. A price is a number that condenses, into a single legible figure, the answer the rest of the economy is currently giving to the question, *what is this worth?* Iron has a price. Labour has a price. Timber has a price. Each of those prices is shaped, in turn, by the prices of everything they can be turned into.

This is what makes the labyrinth above legible. The amber markers along the path are prices. They tell the planner, at every junction, which way the rest of the economy is voting. The pawn does not need a global map; it needs only the marker it is standing next to.

## What disappears

In a fully socialised economy, the means of production are not bought and sold. Iron is not exchanged for timber; both belong to the commune. There are no transactions, hence no transaction prices, hence no numbers that condense the rest of the economy's evaluation into a legible figure. The planner stands at a junction with no marker.

The labyrinth's "without prices" mode shows what this looks like. The walls flicker. The markers fade. The pawn wanders. The point is not that the planner is lazy or that the goal is unimportant. The point is that the *information that would tell the planner which way to go is no longer being produced*.

Where there is no free market, there is no pricing mechanism; without a pricing mechanism, there is no economic calculation.
</PullQuote>

## The Lange–Lerner reply

The most serious response to Mises came from Oskar Lange and Abba Lerner in the 1930s. <Citation n={1}>See Hayek, ed., *Collectivist Economic Planning* (1935), for the contemporaneous Austrian rejoinders.</Citation> They proposed that planners could *imitate* market prices through trial-and-error tâtonnement, adjusting accounting prices upward when surpluses appeared and downward when shortages did. The thing the market does, the argument went, the planner can simulate.

Hayek's reply turned on what the market is actually doing. <Citation n={2}>Hayek, *The Use of Knowledge in Society* (1945), AER 35(4).</Citation> Real prices respond to the local, partial, often inarticulate knowledge held by the people closest to the situation — the harbour-master who knows the tide, the foreman who knows the machine. A planner can only adjust accounting prices in response to data already aggregated and reported, which loses precisely the dispersed knowledge the market is good at gathering.

Salerno later argued, retrieving Mises's original emphasis, that the issue is not knowledge but appraisement: prices are not aggregated information but *acts* of forward-looking entrepreneurial judgment, and a planner cannot perform those acts because they require the discipline of risking one's own capital. <Citation n={3}>Salerno, "Mises and Hayek Dehomogenized," in *RAE* 6(2), 1993; reply in *RAE* 7(2), 1994.</Citation>

## Why this still matters

The full Lange–Lerner socialism is no longer a live political programme; the calculation problem might therefore look like a settled chapter. It is not. Every government decision to fix prices, every accounting regime that imposes a single number on a heterogeneous market, every artificial intelligence pitched as a substitute for price discovery, runs into a smaller version of the same wall.

What you have just watched the pawn fail to do is what every central planner — partial or total, well-meaning or otherwise — must do whenever they intervene without using the market's own coin. The labyrinth above is not a metaphor. It is the situation, scaled down to one decision-maker.

*Homo agit*. Man acts. He acts, when he acts, by reading a price. Take the prices away and the action becomes guesswork.
