A garden is a system of strangers cooperating without a manager. The seeds, the soil, the bees, the rain, the gardener's neighbour who lent the spade — none of them coordinate by reading a plan. They coordinate, when they coordinate, by **prices**: the rent on the land, the wage of the labour, the interest on the loan, the going rate for tomatoes in August. Each price condenses what tens of thousands of dispersed people happen to know into a number a single person can act on.

This is the puzzle Hayek set in 1945. *The Use of Knowledge in Society* is the most concentrated statement we have of why a market is a kind of intelligence and not merely a marketplace. The intelligence does not live in any one head. It lives in the relative prices, and the prices are kept honest by the unit they are quoted in.

## A signal you can edit

The signal can be edited. The signal *has* been edited, throughout monetary history, whenever an authority controlling the unit of account has had a reason to expand the supply. Mises's *Theory of Money and Credit* (1912) names the mechanism precisely: when new money enters circulation, it does not enter everywhere at once. It enters at particular points — banks, governments, favoured borrowers — and from those points it diffuses outward, lifting some prices before others. The first holders of the new money trade at the old price level; the last holders trade at the new one. <Citation n={1}>Mises, *Theory of Money and Credit*, pp. 237–271.</Citation>

This is the **Cantillon effect**, and Mises's contribution was to embed it in a theory of capital. New money does not simply re-distribute purchasing power. It distorts the *relative* prices that producers consult to decide what to make. Cheap credit pulls down the visible cost of long-term projects. Plans that would not have looked profitable at the honest interest rate suddenly look profitable at the artificial one. Resources move toward those plans. Capital structures lengthen — not because saving has lengthened them, but because the discount applied to the future has been quietly cut.

## The garden, briefly green

The early phase looks like growth. The garden is briefly green. Water — the reservoir of real saving — appears to be ample, because someone is pouring water from elsewhere. Grass grows fast. Production nodes pulse vigorously. Even the trees, the long-cycle capital, throw out new branches. <Citation n={2}>Mises, *Human Action*, ch. XVII §§4–6.</Citation>

But the water is being borrowed from the rest of the system, and the new growth has been planted on a forecast that does not match the soil. Lachmann's contribution to this picture is the heterogeneity of capital: not every tool fits every task, and a misread interest rate fits new investment to imaginary preferences. <Citation n={3}>Lachmann, *Capital and Its Structure*, ch. III.</Citation> The longer the distortion runs, the more committed the misallocation becomes — and the harder it is to reverse without breakage.

The most significant fact about this system is the economy of knowledge with which it operates, or how little the individual participants need to know in order to be able to take the right action.
</PullQuote>

## When the signal loses meaning

What looks at first like surplus is, on the books, accumulating fragility. Rothbard's *What Has Government Done to Our Money?* (1963) is the shortest catalogue of what comes next. Inflation reaches consumer prices; the central authority responds either by tightening, which exposes the malinvestment, or by accelerating, which destroys the unit of account altogether. <Citation n={4}>Rothbard, *What Has Government Done*, pt. III.</Citation>

The distortion is not visible from the inside while it is happening. That is what makes it so dangerous: the new money's first effects are indistinguishable, locally, from genuine prosperity. The dead zones — patches of capital committed to projects that never had real demand — appear only when the artificial interest rate is withdrawn and the discount rate snaps back. Hayek called this revelation the *coordination problem*: the question is not whether participants are intelligent, but whether the signal they are using carries the truth.

## The implication

If prices are the medium by which strangers coordinate, then a stable unit of account is not a *technical* concern. It is the precondition of coordination at all. An economy whose money signal can be debased on demand is one in which the entire structure of patient, long-cycle production runs on a wager — that the signal will hold long enough to repay itself. When the wager fails, what fails is not just a project. It is the willingness of strangers to plan with each other.

There is a reason the Austrians keep returning to this point. *Homo agit*: man acts. He acts, when he acts, by reading a price. Garbage in, garbage out — and the garbage in this case grows in the garden you can see above.
