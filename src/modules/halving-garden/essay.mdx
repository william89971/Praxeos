The garden above you is a piece of monetary history done in ink.

Every organism on the page is a Bitcoin block. Its form is a deterministic
function of its hash; its position is given by its height within a halving
epoch. Five epochs, five illuminated pages, seventeen years of proof-of-work.
What you are looking at is not a data visualisation. It is the chain itself,
laid flat.

It is useful to remember, before the theory, how unlikely this object would
have seemed to anyone in 2008. A network of volunteers running identical
software had, by agreement, committed to issue a finite supply of a digital
token on a fixed schedule — fifty tokens every ten minutes for four years,
then twenty-five, then twelve and a half, and so on, until the year
approximately 2140, when the issuance would stop. No authority could change
this. No executive order. No central bank. No Nobel laureate. No one.

The rule is in the code. The code is run by strangers. The strangers have
no reason to obey the code except that they expect other strangers to obey
the same code. It worked anyway.

## The regression theorem

To see why this worked, return to 1892 and <SmallCaps>carl menger</SmallCaps>.
In *On the Origins of Money*, Menger proposed that money had emerged, long
ago and across many cultures, by the same mechanism: people preferred to
trade for the most *saleable* commodity in their local barter network,
because they knew they could trade it onward. Sell-ability begets
sell-ability. The winner-take-most dynamic converged on a single monetary
medium without any decree.

In 1912 <SmallCaps>ludwig von mises</SmallCaps> extended Menger's
observation into a philosophical argument about the *value* of money. Money
has purchasing power today because it had purchasing power yesterday; its
purchasing power yesterday is grounded in its purchasing power the day
before. But this regression cannot run forever — at some point in the
past, what is now money must have been valued for non-monetary reasons:
gold for ornament, shells for beauty, cowries for counting. That original
commodity demand is the anchor.

<PullQuote cite="Mises, Theory of Money and Credit (1912)">
  Before an economic good begins to function as money, it must already
  possess exchange value based on some other cause than its monetary
  function.
</PullQuote>

This is the *regression theorem*, and for a century it was taken to rule
out a pure fiat money from emerging spontaneously. A digital token with no
prior commodity use could not, on this reading, acquire monetary value.

Bitcoin appeared to violate the theorem. It did not. The protocol's early
users were cryptography hobbyists — the cypherpunk list, the Bitcointalk
forum, a handful of academics — for whom the thing had non-monetary value:
it was a cryptographic curiosity, a proof-of-concept, a gesture. That
curiosity-value seeded the process. By the time enough people had begun to
accept Bitcoin for non-trivial transactions, its regression was already in
place. Menger would have recognised the pattern.

## Rules versus discretion

<SmallCaps>f. a. hayek</SmallCaps>'s *Denationalisation of Money* appeared
in 1976, at a grim moment for monetary theory: the Bretton Woods system had
dissolved, the American central bank was discovering how difficult
discretionary management of fiat money actually was, and Hayek — surveying
the wreckage — proposed the most radical response available. The state
monopoly on money, he argued, was the problem. Competing private monies,
each with its own issuance rule, would outperform any central plan.

<Marginalia label="Cross-reference">
  Hayek's <em>Denationalisation</em> was published by the IEA as a tract;
  it was intentionally short, intentionally provocative, and — in 1976 —
  intentionally unserious-seeming. Three decades later, Bitcoin was already
  doing the thing Hayek had proposed, and one of his more eccentric-looking
  pamphlets became a founding text of the space.
</Marginalia>

The halving schedule is the Hayekian gesture made concrete. Monetary policy
for Bitcoin is a cryptographic rule, not a committee. The four halvings
visible in the garden — 2012, 2016, 2020, 2024 — are not events that were
decided; they are events that *occurred*, automatically, in the same way
that the sun rose that day. Executive discretion is absent by construction.

## Stock-to-flow

The twenty-first century's principal contemporary treatment of Bitcoin as
money is <SmallCaps>saifedean ammous</SmallCaps>'s *The Bitcoin Standard*
(2018). Ammous frames the argument around the **stock-to-flow ratio**: the
existing supply of a monetary good divided by its annual production. A high
ratio means new supply cannot dilute existing holders quickly. Gold's
stock-to-flow of about sixty-two is the highest of any pre-digital money
and the principal reason gold retained monetary status across four
millennia.

Every halving doubles Bitcoin's stock-to-flow, mechanically. The first
halving raised it past silver. The second past gold. The subsequent
halvings carry it toward the asymptote: no new supply at all after roughly
2140. The garden you are panning through is a monument to this schedule
because the schedule is its armature — the organism density in each epoch
panel follows the shrinking subsidy as surely as tree-ring density follows
rainfall.

## Sound money, seen

<SmallCaps>murray rothbard</SmallCaps>'s *What Has Government Done to Our
Money?* (1963) is the shortest good introduction to the Austrian monetary
tradition, and its answer to the question in its title is straightforward:
the state takes private commodity money, monopolises its issuance, removes
its commodity backing, and then debases it. Every fiat collapse in history
follows this arc; no fiat currency has escaped it.

Sound money, in the Austrian sense, is money whose issuance is constrained
by something other than a political authority's preference. Historically
that constraint was the cost of extracting gold from the ground. Bitcoin
proposes an alternative: the cost of electricity and time committed to
solving a cryptographic puzzle that nobody needs solved for any other
purpose. The puzzles are meaningless; the commitment is real; the schedule
is the record.

The halving is what the schedule looks like when you plot it. The garden
is what the record looks like when you give it to a botanist.
