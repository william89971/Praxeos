An orchard is not a forest. The trees in a forest grow where they fell as seeds; the trees in an orchard stand where someone wanted them. But once they are planted, the orchard does its own work: the bees, the rain, the fungal mycelium underneath, the neighbour who lent the spade. None of these read a plan. They read each other.

This is the Austrian picture of an economy. Not a forest, not a plan — an orchard whose order is upstream of any individual's design.

## The act

Mises begins where there is no use beginning anywhere else. *Human Action* opens with the action axiom: a person acts to remove a felt uneasiness, choosing among means in pursuit of ends. <Citation n={1}>Mises, *Human Action*, ch. I.</Citation> The axiom is not a hypothesis; it is the precondition for there being anything to hypothesise about. To deny it, you have to act — and so reaffirm it.

Each act is, in that minimal sense, *information*. It is information about what the actor preferred at that instant, given what they expected and what they knew. Every purchase declares a price one was willing to pay. Every refusal declares a price one was not. The orchard above you is the picture of those declarations: each cypress is one actor, each pulse is one act.

## The signal

The signal does not stop at the actor who emits it. It travels, attenuated, through whatever network connects them to others. The classic statement of why this matters is Hayek's 1945 paper. <Citation n={2}>Hayek, *The Use of Knowledge in Society*, AER 35(4).</Citation> No central planner, however well-intentioned, can collect the dispersed knowledge that exists in the population. Knowledge of the particular circumstances of time and place — that the lumber yard has a backlog, that the river is low, that the neighbour will trade pears for cider — exists only in the heads of those who have it.

What enables strangers to act on this dispersed knowledge is the price system. The price is the radio signal, scaled across millions of receivers, of what other people are choosing. Hayek's contribution was to insist that this is not a metaphor. It is the literal mechanism by which a complex society coordinates without a coordinator.

<PullQuote attribution="Hayek, 1945">
The most significant fact about this system is the economy of knowledge with which it operates, or how little the individual participants need to know in order to be able to take the right action.
</PullQuote>

## The alertness

Kirzner adds a third element: alertness. <Citation n={3}>Kirzner, *Competition and Entrepreneurship*, ch. 2.</Citation> Markets do not equilibrate because actors solve a maximization problem with full information. They equilibrate because some actors notice opportunities others have missed — discrepancies between what is offered here and what is sought there. The entrepreneur is not the inventor or the financier in this picture; the entrepreneur is the one who sees what is already there, in the price differentials that everyone else has overlooked.

This makes the orchard above you something more than a transmission medium. The actors who light up are not just relaying. Some of them are *re-evaluating*. Each pulse may be amplified or dampened by what its receivers already know. Coordination is not the product of a finished computation; it is the running residue of countless small re-evaluations.

## The heterogeneity

Lachmann's emphasis cuts the other way. <Citation n={4}>Lachmann, *The Market as an Economic Process*.</Citation> Not every actor reads the signal the same way; not every signal carries the same content; not every act fits every other act. The market process is not a fast convergence to equilibrium — it is an open-ended pattern of partial coordination interrupted by genuine novelty. The orchard above you is, in this picture, never finished.

What you should leave with is a feeling for what the price system *is*. It is not a thermostat reading the temperature of demand. It is the network the orchard above you suggests: a pattern of partial illuminations, each carrying the trace of an act, none of them complete, all of them readable by whoever has learned to read them.

*Homo agit*. Man acts. The rest is what we call the economy.
