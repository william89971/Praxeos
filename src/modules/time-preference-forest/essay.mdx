The forest above you is a piece of Austrian capital theory done in ink.

Every tree is a production structure. Its canopy is the stream of consumer
goods that structure yields. Its root system is the real capital on which
the canopy rests. The two move together — not because the ratio is dictated
from above, but because trees that grow a canopy larger than their roots can
support are trees that will fall.

That is the plain version of the theory. The formal version is older. In
1884 <SmallCaps>eugen von böhm-bawerk</SmallCaps> published the first
volume of *Kapital und Kapitalzins*; over the next three decades he and
his students built a capital theory that located interest not in
exploitation (Marx's answer) but in the systematic valuation of present
goods over future goods. People are, on average and other things equal,
impatient; they would rather have a meal now than a meal next week. The
rate at which they trade future against present — time preference — is the
pure economic origin of interest.

<Marginalia label="Source">
  Böhm-Bawerk, *Capital and Interest* (1884). The pure time-preference theory
  of interest is a foundation stone of Austrian economics; it dissolves the
  Marxian account without recourse to empirical detail.
</Marginalia>

The second Böhm-Bawerkian insight — the one the forest makes visible — is
**roundaboutness**. A capital-rich economy does not produce consumer goods
directly. It produces tools that produce tools that produce consumer goods.
This *Produktionsumweg*, production detour, is more productive per unit of
labour than direct production would be, but only if the requisite savings
exist to sustain the intermediate stages while they are being built. A
longer production structure requires lower time preference: more patience,
more saving, more willingness to defer consumption through the interval
when the tools being made are not yet producing their yield.

willing to wait. What remains are stumps and quick weeds: short-chain
production, present consumption, no depth.

## The intervention

Now raise the central-bank intervention slider. An orange glow appears
beneath the canopy. The trees grow — upward, above their previous height.
But if you look below ground, nothing has changed. The root systems are
exactly what they were. The added canopy is resting on capital that
does not exist.

This is <SmallCaps>f. a. hayek</SmallCaps>'s argument from *Prices and Production*
(1931), extending <SmallCaps>ludwig von mises</SmallCaps>'s 1912 account of
credit-induced cycles in *The Theory of Money and Credit*. When the central
bank suppresses the market interest rate below the rate that would have
prevailed under actual saving — when it creates credit out of nothing — it
sends entrepreneurs the signal that long-structure production is more
profitable than it is. They invest in production detours that the
underlying capital stock cannot sustain.

For a time this looks like boom. The statistics are good; asset prices
rise; employment is high; the canopy shades the forest floor. But the
intervention does not generate the savings required to complete those
production structures. The forest has been drawn larger than it is.

## The correction

Press the market-correction button. The orange dissipates. The artificial
growth — the branches that existed only because of credit expansion — turns
black. And the trees, those that had grown beyond the capacity of their
roots to hold them, fall.

The eight seconds you are watching are not a natural disaster. They are
the market doing what the market does: revealing, after some lag, the
economic truth that the intervention had concealed. <SmallCaps>murray
rothbard</SmallCaps>'s *America's Great Depression* (1963) applies this
framework to the 1920s. The eighty years since have supplied additional
examples.

<Marginalia label="Note">
  The cascade is choreographed, but the underlying model is not decorative:
  tree height is a function of (time preference × savings × intervention),
  and the correction simply sets intervention to zero and lets the forest
  relax to the height its roots can actually sustain.
</Marginalia>

## The savings slider

After the correction, a new slider appears: savings rate. Raise it. The
root systems deepen. The mycorrhizal network — the Lachmannian capital
structure — re-appears and thickens. The canopy rises, this time with the
roots beneath it to hold it up. The recovery is slow, which is why real
recoveries are slow: capital takes time to build, and no amount of monetary
stimulus shortens the interval.

<PullQuote cite="Hayek, Prices and Production, 1931">
  Every rate of interest which is lower than the rate that would establish
  equilibrium between saving and investment leads necessarily to a…
  disproportionate development of the stages of production.
</PullQuote>

There is a more austere moral. You can grow the canopy of an economy with
cheap credit. You cannot grow its roots. Any gap between the two is a
measure of the eventual correction. The Austrian argument does not say
that booms are illusions; it says that the *length* of a boom is a
measure of how deep the eventual disappointment will be. The forest shows
this with thirty lines of ink.
